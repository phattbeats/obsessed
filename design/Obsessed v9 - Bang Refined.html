§ 1 · The diagnosis

From a distance, the wedge IS Trivial Pursuit.

A six-slice pie + saturated primary palette + thick white outlines is the most recognizable trivia game silhouette ever printed. We borrowed it on purpose to say "trivia night" — but the cost is that Obsessed reads as a TP knock-off before the wordmark has a chance to land. That's a brand-equity tax we shouldn't pay, especially since our actual product isn't a coffee-table almanac of facts. It's a dossier on someone you know. The visual language should match that.

Our current mark (v2)
Six slices, primaries + secondaries, thick white outlines.
A generic 6-slice trivia pie
Different colors. Same silhouette. Same read.
The fix isn't to swap colors. It's to retire the pie entirely. The wedge needs to do three jobs — logo mark, 0–6 scoring indicator, and round-end celebration — but none of them require a circle cut into six pieces. Below are five directions that solve all three without borrowing TP's silhouette. Each leans into the dossier / hyper-personal investigation story that's already baked into the rest of the product (the profile editor, the Special Elite labels, the "tonight's victim" framing on the host TV).
§ 2 · Five directions

One brand object, three jobs — five ways.

Each direction gives a logo lockup, a favicon ladder (16 / 24 / 32 / 64), and the scoring mechanic that replaces the wedge — sized first as a board, then in the player's pocket. Tagline copy is placeholder.

Why this works

The dossier metaphor is already in the product — the profile editor uses Special Elite labels and a red corner stamp. Promoting it to the main brand tightens the system instead of inventing a new one. The "!" mark is unique, ownable, and scales to favicon. Round-end animation (a stamp landing with ink spatter) is more satisfying than a wedge filling in.

Trade-off: shifts the app's overall surface toward warm paper tones — either the whole UI moves to that palette, or we keep the dark-navy app and treat dossier stamps as an inserted "evidence layer."

Why this works

The most divergent direction. Treats the game as surveillance output rather than party trivia, which fits the "we scraped their Reddit" reality of the product more honestly than the playful pie ever did. Also the cheapest to animate (height tween only).

Trade-off: Pretty stark for a social party game. Probably scares off casual players. Best as a "host mode" or admin-view inflection, not the main consumer surface.

RECOMMENDED PATH

Pick one of 01 · Case File, 02 · Corkboard, or 04 · Bang.

These three solve the TP problem cleanly and each has a real shot at owning a visual lane in the trivia category. 04 (Bang) is the lowest-risk path — it keeps the existing app palette and only swaps the brand object, so we can ship it in a sprint. 01 (Case File) is the boldest brand statement — it commits to the dossier metaphor everywhere and would let us write better copy throughout. 02 (Corkboard) is the most playful and the most "scrolly, physical, fun."

The other two are useful as secondary visual languages — 03 (Highlighter) is a great pattern for the post-game readout screen, and 05 (Readout) is a great host-admin / debug overlay aesthetic. Don't waste them.

  1. Pick a primary. I'd put it on 04 unless you want the brand to be louder than the gameplay.
  2. Adapt the wedge component's API to the new mark — the controller methods (fillCategory, setFills, isComplete) all transfer 1:1.
  3. Update the host TV hero first; it's where the TP collision is most embarrassing.
  4. Defer favicon + app icon until the mark is locked — there's no point pixel-fitting twice.
